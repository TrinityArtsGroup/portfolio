Acryllic / Collage Examples

These are a few examples of my work in acrylics and collage. I have been working in this medium since 2020.

"...Ahab Stands Alone" (2020)

  • This piece was inspired by the novel "Moby Dick" by Herman Melville. It is my brother's favorite
    book and I wanted to create something that he would appreciate. The piece is acryllic on canvas, and
    is three dimensional in nature. It utilizes driftwood, seashells and the remains of a horseshoe crab
    found all on location in Orleans, MA in September of 2020.

"Crime of the Century" (2019) - First Version

  • This piece was inspired by a screenplay entitled "Jafsie" that is based on the infamous 1932 Lindbergh
    Kidnapping Case that was told from an unorthodox point of view: that of the go-between for both the
    kidnappers and the Lindbergh's and the kidnappers - a 78 year old Bronx school Principal named Dr. John
    F. Condon (J.F.C). It was the first canvas that I ever designed. Utilizing all kinds of mixed media,
    from vellum and acrylic to cardstock. Above is the first version from 2019, which was gifted to the
    co-author of the screenplay, Mark Lechner.

"Crime of the Century" (2020) - Second Version

  • This is the second version of the piece, which I kept for myself. It is a more refined version of the
    original, and was created in 2020. Whereas, the first version focused primarily on the newspaper media's
    cavalier attitude and the nature of the America's first celebrity, this second version puts a spotlight
    directly on little Charlie himself, and how his life got lost in the maelstrom.

"Forgive Dallas" (2021)

  • This piece was inspired by the 1963 assassination of President John F. Kennedy, a favorite subject
    of poet Tohm Bakelas, of which this was a commission. It is a collage piece similar to the Lindbergh
    collage, but it pushes even further - integrating newspaper clippings, photo paper, vellum, acryllic,
    16mm film strips, dye, and caro syrup. It even utilizes several vintage Kennedy political buttons.
    Below is the poet proudly displaying the finished canvas.
